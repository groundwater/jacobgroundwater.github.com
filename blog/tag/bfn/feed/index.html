This is not introductory material in mathematics

It is expected that to enjoy this posting, once has a firm grasp of what a function is, as well as how they are applied in mathematics, specifically calculus.

A followup introductory will be eventually posted, aimed at those who have little to no math experience outside of basic arithmetic — please be patient.

Foreword

I am presenting what I call block function notation or BFN for short.

I believe BFN is clearer, easier to learn and fits more naturally with computational mathematics. It basically turns this \[ f(g(x)) \] into this \[ x\ \big|\ f\ \big|\ g \]

Goals

  1. to create a notation that is intuitive for both beginners and non-beginners
  2. to avoid conflicting existing notation whenever possible
  3. to be as explicit as possible
  4. to admit advanced ideas easily into the system
  5. to work well with numerical an computational mathematics

Introduction

Functions, Functions, Function

Abstractions are the fuel of mathematics, and the function is an abstraction matched by few others. In a world of petrol fuelled racing cars, functions are a 200 tonne solid booster rocket strapped to a volkswagen beetle. A good driver will get you to the finish line before the other cars have even changed gears.

My goal is to emphasize how powerful functions are as a tool, but equally how dangerous they can be. When I say dangerous I mean they are too powerful an idea for many people to handle, even university students.

The Good, The Bad, The Ugly

The following are not functions :

  • \( x+1 \)
  • \( x^2 - 2x - 1\)
  • \( \frac{d}{dx}e^{-x^2} \)

The former are expressions which are very much not functions. This distinction is subtle and often missed in math courses. It's overlooked because math goes between the two so often it's simply not relevant to a students ability to pass the exam.

A function is often a cog in a much larger program, programming languages understand this that is why they have compilers. A compiler typically checks that the output of one function is allowed to be input into the next. When a program has thousands or millions of function calls, this cannot be checked with anything other than a computer.

Function signatures matter, i.e. the types of values that functions accept and return. In traditional notation we define a function \(f\) by \[ f : \mathbb{R}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}\] This implies \(f\ \) accepts a real number as an argument and returns a value of the same type. This notation isn't too bad, despite my personal dislike. The real danger comes in the next step, when defining the function explicitly. How many calculus textbooks write something like the following \[\begin{align}f : \mathbb{R}\rightarrow\mathbb{R} \\ f(x) = x^2\end{align}\] What exactly is occurring in the second line? Is \(f(x)\) a function, expression or something else? If we assume that \(x^2\) cannot possibly represent a function then by equality \(f(x)\) is not a function. Its just an odd statement to have lying around. It gets worse when realizing that, in calculus, we often act like \(f(x)\) represents a function.

My suggestion is as follow

\[ \begin{align}
f =& \mathbb{R}\Rightarrow\mathbb{R} : \\
&x\rightarrow x^2
\end{align}\]

I hope there is less confusion about what each piece is. \(f\) is definitely a function, \(x\) is some real number. It shouldn't be too hard to figure out that \(x\rightarrow x^2\) represents the behaviour of the function.

Functions in Action

Using our new notation let's get started

\[\begin{align}
f =& \mathbb{Z} \Rightarrow \mathbb{R} :\\
& x \rightarrow \frac{1}{2}x
\end{align} \]

and

\[\begin{align}
g= & \mathbb{Z^+} \Rightarrow \mathbb{Z} :\\
& x \rightarrow -x
\end{align} \]

Then \( g | f  \) is a function with signature
\[ \mathbb{Z^+}\Rightarrow\mathbb{R} \]
normally written as \( f(g(x))\).

Why this new notation \( g|f \)? If we replace \(g\) and \(f\) with their signatures the usefulness becomes much clearer \[ g | f \text{ or } (\mathbb{Z^+}\Rightarrow\mathbb{Z}) \big| (\mathbb{Z}\Rightarrow\mathbb{R})\] which becomes \[ \mathbb{Z^+}\Rightarrow\mathbb{R}  \] The terms no either side of the vertical slash \(\big|\) must agree (specifically the left-hand-side must be a subset of the right-hand-side). The center terms essentially cancel out, leaving a type signature of only the outer types. We can create long compositions of functions this way with clarity.
